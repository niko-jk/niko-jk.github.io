ARE PRISONS OBSOLETE?

ARE PRISONS OBSOLETE?

*Davis, Angela Y., Are Prisons Obsolete? New York: Seven Stories Press, 2003

Imagine a world without prisons or police.
It’s hard, right?

When I imagine a world without prisons or police, I imagine a world where #blacklivesmatter.
Maybe you’re asking: “What do Black people have to do with prisons and police?”
Leave this site now.
Maybe you’re not asking that.
When I say:
abolish prisons
or
defund the police
you want to know what we should do instead.
Fair enough. Why throw the baby out with the bathwater?

We all of us are entangled in racist institutions and practices. Our country was built on the backs of slaves and immigrants that we have continued to “denigrate” openly and control through more subtle enculturation. This is not a “one bad apple” problem. This is about recognizing the racist underpinnings of the institutions that control our population through force and realizing that it is all of our responsibilities to expose and end a culture of systemic psychological and physical violence against minorities in our country.
There is a piece I come back to time and time again from my friend Mariame. Mariame Kaba is the founder of Project Nia and a badass blogger of Prison Culture.   When people ask me what prison abolition is, I inevitably give them a much less articulate version of MK’s description here:

Defining Prison Abolition

Scholar-activist Ruthie Gilmore has defined abolition as “a movement to end systemic violence, including the interpersonal vulnerabilities and displacements that keep the system going.” Practically, that looks like “creating structures that reduce the demand and need for prisons,” as my friend and colleague Erica Meiners has written. She adds:
“It is ensuring that communities have viable, at least living-wage, jobs that are not dehumanizing. It means establishing mechanisms for alternative dispute resolution and other processes that address conflict or harm with mediation. It means ensuring that our most vulnerable populations, for example those who are mentally ill or undereducated, do not get warehoused in our prisons and jails because of the failure of other institutions such as health care and education.”
And no matter how many times people ask what I do as a prison abolitionist, I’ve never found a more elegant or concise way to voice my ambitions than the words MK shared in this, my favorite blog post:
Taking Action As there is no blueprint for abolition, we must spend time imagining, strategizing, and practicing other futures. In my work this encompasses many facets: We organize and mobilize to address the root causes of oppression and violence. We test the limits of our imagination of what’s possible in terms of addressing violence and harm. We creatively rethink our current structures of policing and warehousing individuals. We expose the brutality and abject failure of the current system. We foreground a revolutionary transformation of ideas while demanding that our resources be radically reallocated. Collectively envisioned and determined, abolition will look different from one community to the next
Last year, Mychal Denzel Smith wrote a great piece dealing with just this question: “Abolish the Police. Instead, Let’s Have Full Social, Economic, and Political Equality” (The Nation, April 9, 2015). He wrote:
The police [and I’ll add prisons here] are not performing the function we say they are, and there are real ways to achieve a world with less violence that don’t include the police [or prisons]. We simply haven’t tried.
And:
This isn’t about getting “better” police, ones who exercise discretion in using force, but getting away from “needing” police altogether.
He ends, saying:
My honest answer is that I don’t know what a world without police looks like. I only know there will be less dead black people. I know that a world without police is a world with one less institution dedicated to the maintenance of white supremacy and inequality.It’s a world worth imagining.
